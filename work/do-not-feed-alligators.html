Do Not Feed Alligators (2024)

birch wood, acrylic sheet, acrylic paint, foam clay, 3D-printed tart, microcontrollers, mini OLED screen, thermal printer, LEDs, sensors, motors, magnets

Created in response to the New York café Do Not Feed Alligators, this portable animatronic altar pays irreverent homage to a fictional snack deity. Half shrine, half joke, the box-like sculpture blends absurd devotion with speculative interactivity.

Upon approaching the altar, a human eye appears on a small OLED screen—an apparition of the cookie god, triggered by an ultrasonic sensor and eerily reflecting the bartender's gaze. Red LEDs act as minimalist candles, while a golden halo made of painted acrylic outlines the god's presence in a nod to South American religious altars. From the tail of the alligator, a coffee bean periodically drops, animated by a hidden servo motor with magnets. Depending on one's reading, the bean is either an egg (resurrection) or feces (blasphemy)—a visual pun that mirrors the sacred/absurd ambiguity of the tart on display.

The second half of the altar presents a fruit tart, concealing tactile buttons inside its berries. When pressed, they activate a thermal printer that delivers randomized oracles: cryptic affirmations or denials about whether the tart may be eaten. Beneath it lies a piece of gospel sheet music—except the hymn is Schnappi das kleine Krokodil, the children's song of a tiny crocodile, completing this ritual of playful profanation.
