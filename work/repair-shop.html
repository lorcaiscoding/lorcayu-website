Repair Shop (2025)

co-developed with Kathleen McDermott (as RA and collaborator)
exhibited at Open Source Gallery, Brooklyn
found appliances, motors, fabric, sensors, microcontrollers, foam, paint

Repair Shop is a solo exhibition by artist Kathleen McDermott. It turns the gallery into an experimental space for considering the lifespan of objects and when they are assigned "value," especially in the context of work. Encouraging audience engagement and collaboration, Repair Shop evolves over the course of the exhibition.

One side of the gallery is set up as a showroom, featuring appliances from McDermott's home that can no longer perform their original functions—a sewing machine that doesn't sew well, a blender that leaks motor fluid—but that still have some working parts. Using techniques from puppetry and animatronics, McDermott has made strange, campy attachments for these "broken" machines, expanding on the parts that do work and giving them new use. The bright pink showroom borrows from 1950s advertising tropes of domestic appliances, where machines were described in terms of gendered labor and sold with promises of domestic productivity.

The other side of the gallery is configured as an absurdist repair shop. Community members are invited to donate formerly precious electronic objects and other heirlooms for "repair." If an object is accepted, the owners acknowledge that its form and function will be significantly altered. In advance of, and throughout the exhibition, McDermott and a rotating group of artist-collaborators rework found and donated objects using paint, fabric, motors, and inflation, transforming them into a cast of animated object "characters" that pose questions around work, value, and the labor of repair.

I worked closely with McDermott as a collaborator and research assistant throughout the development of Repair Shop. My contributions focused on the design and fabrication of several structural, kinetic, and electronic components for the exhibition—most notably the modified sewing machine and other animated appliances. My work included rapid prototyping, structural modeling, sensor integration, interaction design, 3D modeling and printing, laser cutting, CNC milling, painting, sanding, and on-site assembly. These collaborative processes formed an extension of the exhibition's central themes: hybrid labor, value transformation, and the poetics of dysfunctional objects.
