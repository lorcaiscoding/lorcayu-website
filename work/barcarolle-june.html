Barcarolle: June (2024)

projector, voile, air purifier

My favorite piece of Tchaikovsky is "Barcarolle: June" from his The Seasons suite. I started learning the accordion at the age of five, and this was one of the earliest pieces I mastered, as well as one of my earliest memories of Tchaikovsky. The music is filled with a sense of instability, always giving me a drifting, ethereal feeling — like water, like memory. Yet it also brings a sense of security. I often listen to it as I fall asleep, as it makes me imagine being curled up in a cabin during a night voyage. Since coming to US from across the ocean, I always recall the line: "My homeland cannot follow my wishes, and this foreign land cannot ease my fatigue." I increasingly feel as though I can never stand on solid ground, always drifting and swaying on the water. I have no roots, nowhere to belong. There is no place for me on the continents.

An image of a cherrywood chair with a backrest was projected onto a thin piece of voile, with some wavy folds at the bottom of the fabric, hinting that this is a water-related scene. Beneath the voile, an air purifier was placed to create some fluttering and swaying, adding an element of instability. Then, in rhythm with the music, the sharpness of the chair was adjusted through the projector's focal length, implying the changes in distance. In fact, the chair is the only stable object in the entire installation, yet it remains empty, with no one seated. An identical chair is projected onto the wall behind it, but this one is static, large, solid, and flat — an allegory of continent.
