All the Rivers Sound in My Body (2022)

ArcGIS, scanned palm lines

Inspired by Pablo Neruda's verse "All the rivers sound in my body" (Queen), this project explores how marks—bodily, geographic, and symbolic—mediate the space between self and world, memory and terrain. Created in my hometown Wuhan, the "city of a thousand lakes," during the third year of COVID-19 lockdowns, the work draws on ArcGIS to gradually transform the lines of a palm—an intimate and irreplicable mark of identity—into a fictional watershed. The process reimagines the body as both terrain and archive, tracing how meaning is reshaped when ordinary life is put on hold.

What begins as a record of the body becomes a shifting topography shaped by crisis and the slow erosion of daily meaning. The work reflects on how personal and collective histories flow into one another—especially in times of suspension and rupture. In this layered interplay of skin, software, and imagined geography, it asks how invisible forces leave their traces, and how we come to understand place, time, and self when the coordinates of normal life dissolve. The resulting terrain is neither fully real nor imagined, but suspended—like a river—between presence and disappearance.
